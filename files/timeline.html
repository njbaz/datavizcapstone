A Brief History of Standardized Testing

Standardized testing in the U.S. has historically been tied to systemic inequities, with roots in early 20th-century intelligence tests shaped by eugenics. These assessments often perpetuated racial and socioeconomic biases by privileging cultural norms of dominant groups. Over time, tools like the SAT and policies like NCLB institutionalized high-stakes testing, penalizing schools serving marginalized communities and narrowing the definition of student success. These trends highlighted the flaws in treating standardized tests as neutral measures of aptitude or achievement.

Post-COVID, these historical inequities have become even more pronounced. The pandemic widened gaps in access to education, technology, and resources, disproportionately impacting underserved communities. Test scores in this context often reflect systemic barriers rather than individual potential. As schools and institutions move toward holistic evaluations and test-optional policies, there is an urgent need to reconsider the role of standardized testing, ensuring it aligns with equity-focused education systems.
